Culture and the arts

On Nicollet Avenue at 12600 Nicollet Avenue is the Ames Performing Arts Center. Both the Proscenium Stage and the Black Box are located at the center. With a view of the Nicollet Commons Park, Minnesota River Valley, and the Minneapolis skyline, the lobby is a two-story glass structure. In its 190-square-meter (2,000-square-foot) home, the art gallery hosts eight exhibitions a year. Displaying a wide range of artwork from both established and up-and-coming artists is the goal of the art mission. There are also conference rooms and a large rehearsal space in addition to the theaters and gallery. The Ames Center hosts a wide variety of performances, including everything from classical music to comedy, jazz to Christian rock, and everything in between. A wide variety of shows, from comedies to dramas, are on offer. Additional banquet, special event, and reception space is available.

There are no museums in the city, but the Lake Alimagnet Center for the Arts, located near Lake Alimagnet in eastern Burnsville, does provide community space for arts and non-profit organizations. Youth and teen activities are a major focus in Burnsville. The GARAGE Youth Center, a non-profit music club and teen center located near City Hall, has hosted numerous national and international music acts over the years.

The Burnsville Fire Muster is held every year in late August or early September. The event was first held in the 1970s as a short parade and showcase for a local fire-equipment collector, and was relaunched in 1980. The event now includes a large parade, music concerts, and fireworks in the tradition of the New England fire muster. In 2004, the Guinness Book of World Records cited the namesake event of this celebration, a fire truck parade, as the longest of its kind in the world.

Comcast and Time Warner Cable serve Burnsville's residents and businesses with the same television programming, news, and cable channels as the rest of the metro area. Burnsville Community Television (BCTV) is a non-profit organization that broadcasts free, educational, and entertaining programming to the community. For local news, the Burnsville Sun Current and Thisweek Newspapers, as well as the Minneapolis Star Tribune's South section, are all excellent options.

Recreational facilities and open spaces

Mel Larson Field, the BAC's football-only field, is located in the vicinity of Black Dog Power Plant.

The Burnsville Parks Department, which follows a Parks & Trails Master Plan, oversees the city's 1,800 acres (7.3 km2) of parkland. Thirds of the land is untouched, while the rest is developed and used for recreational purposes. The Minnesota Valley National Wildlife Refuge lies along Burnsville's northern border with the Minnesota River. The city's main recreational lake, Crystal Lake, is open to boating, fishing, jet-skiing, and swimming, among other activities. Summer hours at the Burnsville Skate Park are completely free of charge. Two professional ice rinks are available at the Burnsville Ice Center.

Burnsville Athletic Club is an all-volunteer youth sports league for children in the area. Nearly 1,300 players participate in the baseball leagues for grades K-12, 80–90 boys basketball teams for grades 3–12, and more than 400 players in flag and tackle American football for grades 2–8 each year. This year's 8th grade boys' football team travels to compete against other 8th grade teams from across the state in a higher level of competition. The city's recreation department, other recreational organizations, and minor league groups also offer a variety of adult sports.

Transportation

MVTA provides regional bus service between many transit hubs in the city, south of the river, and to destinations such as the Mall of America, Downtown Minneapolis, and Southdale Mall. The majority of residents use automobiles to get to and from work and other destinations. Interstate 35W and 35E split in Burnsville, with Interstate 35W leading to Minneapolis and Interstate 35E leading to St. Paul's downtown. Burnsville Parkway, County Road 42, and State Highway 13 are the primary entrances and exits to I-35W. There is an east–west link between the western suburb of Savage and the eastern suburbs of Eagan and Apple Valley via County Road 42 and State Highway 13. As well as McAndrews Road (E. 138th Street), County Road 5 (Kenwood Trail), County Road 11, Portland Avenue, Southcross Drive and Lac Lavon Road are the most important interior arteries in the township.

West and east sections of the city have retail shopping along County Road 42 and Highway 13, with local shopping nodes scattered throughout. Suburban shopping malls and restaurants line County Road 42, which is the main thoroughfare in the area. Burnsville Center, a 1,275,703-square-foot (118,516.7 m2) regional mall, serves as the strip's centerpiece.

The Mall of America, Valleyfair Amusement Park, Buck Hill Ski Area, the Minneapolis–St. Paul International Airport, downtown Saint Paul, and downtown Minneapolis are all within a 15- to 30-minute drive from Burnsville.. Apple Valley, Bloomington, Eagan, Lakeville, and Savage, all nearby, offer even more shopping opportunities, as well as lakes and green spaces for residents to enjoy.

City's Center

Intelligent urbanism and smart growth inform Burnsville's "Heart of the City" downtown development initiative. Near the Minnesota River in central Burnsville, the redevelopment covers 54 acres of land.

In January 2009, the Ames Performing Arts Center in Burnsville, Minnesota, opened its doors to the public for the first time. It was controversial when it was approved in 2007. At Nicollet Avenue and Burnsville Parkway, a Doron Jensen-signature restaurant is the centerpiece of the Heart of the City project.

Burnsville is a small town in Dakota County, Minnesota, about 15 miles (24 kilometers) south of Minneapolis. Upstream from its confluence with the Mississippi River, the city sits on a bluff overlooking the Minnesota River's south bank. Minnesota's 16th-largest metropolitan area, Minneapolis–Saint Paul, includes the city of Burnsville and its surrounding suburbs. There are 64,317 people in the city as of the 2020 census.

Nearby attractions include the Burnsville Center regional mall, a portion of the Murphy-Hanrehan Park Reserve, the 310-foot-tall Buck Hill ski mountain, and the Minnesota Valley National Wildlife Refuge. Burnsville is also home to several other notable landmarks.

Burnsville was built on the site of a Mdewakanton Dakota village. As time went on, it evolved into a rural Irish farming community. Following the completion of Interstate 35, Burnsville became Minnesota's tenth-largest city in the 2000 Census. By the late 2000s, it had become the ninth-largest suburb in the metro area and a bedroom community for both Minneapolis and St. Paul. With urban-style retail and condominiums, Burnsville's downtown area is known as Heart of the City. Minnesota Valley Transit Authority's headquarters are located at the Burnsville Transit Station, which provides regional bus service to five other nearby cities.

William Byrne, an early Irish settler and landowner, is credited with giving the area the name Burnsville. As it stands, his last name is recorded as "Burns."
